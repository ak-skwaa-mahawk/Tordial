# Toroidal Root — 3D Geometric Framework

## The Flat Circle Problem

Standard π (3.14159...) is derived from a flat, 2D circle. It is the ratio of circumference to diameter on a plane. This is geometrically correct and remains valid for static, flat, non-rotating systems.

The question this framework raises: when you apply a 2D constant to a 3D rotating system, what is the residue? What is the gap between the flat approximation and the real rotating geometry?

## The Torus as Active Shape

A torus is a circle that has gained a third dimension by rotating around an external axis. Unlike a flat circle it has:

- Continuous flow paths — energy and motion can circulate through the center and around the outer edge
- Two radii (R and r) — the major radius and the tube radius
- Volume and thickness from the start — not a flat approximation extended into 3D

Toroidal geometry appears in real physical systems: magnetic field configurations, plasma confinement (tokamak reactors), atmospheric circulation, certain electromagnetic field structures.

## Relationship to Blueprint π

The flat 2D circle (π = 3.14159...) is preserved as the static limiting case — valid when:
- The system is not rotating
- Temperature and pressure are at cold static limits
- No dynamic load is applied

When the system is running — rotating under load, at operating temperature, with real tolerances accumulated — the effective geometric constant shifts from the blueprint value.

Tordial tracks that shift. The toroidal framework is the geometric structure that describes the running state.

## Compatibility

This framework does not invalidate existing equations. It organizes them:

- Flat 2D π remains correct for its domain (static limits)
- Toroidal geometry extends the framework into rotating, dynamic systems
- Existing engineering equations (pressure, voltage, fluid dynamics) are branches that inherit from the appropriate level of this structure
